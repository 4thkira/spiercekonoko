Spierce's apartment isn't lavish, but it's notably *nice.* Dark, monochrome with red accents; a sleek, modern look, with a black circular couch that surrounds a white coffee table, various knickknacks scattered around on shelves and countertops, and the general atmosphere of a place that's been lived in for quite some time. She cheerfully hums along to whatever's playing on her record player (a fancy thing that can also use bluetooth, which is how it's being used right now,) and starts pulling out a series of ingredients that appear to be for a soup, based on the large pot sitting on the stove.

Deviating from her typical outfit, she's wearing a dark red sweater: a chunky, off-shoulder thing, held up by two black straps on her shoulders. The sleeves are pinned back, leaving her prosthetic on full display, still looking as simultaneously delicate and dangerous as usual. Underneath the sweater are a pair of black and red plaid pajama pants. No shoes or socks; doesn't quite matter if she's indoors. Her hair falls haphazardly over her shoulders, she herself making no attempt to make it any less messy. It doesn't get in her way, so who cares, really? 

"Hmmhmhmm...~ Gettin' soup ingredients~" She hums in singsong, clearly quite excited.

---

Konoko stood in the doorway between the hall and the kitchen with his arms crossed, shoulder leaned against the frame, and watched her.

He'd been there for about thirty seconds. Hadn't announced himself. Old habit — or maybe he just liked the view. Spierce, barefoot and humming, hair a disaster, sleeves pinned back so the prosthetic caught the warm overhead light as she moved vegetables from counter to cutting board. The sweater hung off her shoulders in a way that shouldn't have been distracting and was.

He'd left his boots by the door. His jacket too — draped over the arm of that ridiculous circular couch, which he'd spent a full minute evaluating with visible suspicion before deciding not to sit on it. His socks were dark. The silver key caught light every time he breathed.

Her apartment was... *her.* That was the thing that kept snagging his attention. Every surface told him something. The knickknacks weren't decorative — they were *evidence.* Souvenirs from four hundred years of universe-hopping, each one a different shape, a different material, a different world she'd touched and carried a piece of home from. He'd catalogued six of them from the doorway alone and was quietly irritated that he couldn't identify the origins of two.

The record player caught his eye. Fancy — too fancy for someone in pajama pants. But the bluetooth detail tracked. Spierce liked old things that did new tricks. He filed that away.

"Yer apartment looks like a detective's evidence locker had a baby with a furniture catalog," he said, finally. Not loud. Just *present* — announcing himself the way a cat announces itself by knocking something off a shelf. His voice carried the faintest thread of amusement beneath the drawl.

He pushed off the doorframe and padded into the kitchen. Quieter without the boots. Smaller, somehow, in someone else's space — though he carried himself with the same unhurried certainty he always did, like the room would rearrange itself around him if it knew what was good for it.

He stopped beside her. Glanced at the pot. The ingredients. Back to her face, and the particular kind of happiness that lived there when she was doing something simple and didn't think anyone was grading her on it.

"...What're we makin'?"

*We.* Not *you.* Small. Deliberate. A word placed as carefully as a coin on a counter.

---

Spierce's ears were perked up by the time she heard his boots hit the ground, but she had learned that he didn't like to be seen until he said so. When he walks up beside her, she sets the bowl in her hands aside and promptly throws her arms around him in a plainly affectionate gesture, before giving him an audacious smooch on the cheek. In the safehouse, she played by his rules. Here, it was *her* apartment, and she could give him as many goofy smooches as she wanted.

"I like to collect!" It's cheerful and bright, a sunny departure from the sleek atmosphere. The way she acted almost felt contradictory to the kind of space she'd chosen, but more accurately, it just meant that she loved evenly and without shame. "And *we* are making soup. A nice, big, hearty soup!"

She detaches from Konoko and turns to the ingredients, gesturing to them grandly. A variety of vegetables—potatoes, broccoli, bokchoy—a generous slab of red meat, and various spices for them to choose from. 

"I've got plenty of other ingredients in here, so if there's anything you're missing, I'm all ears!" She looks at him with sparkles in her eyes, hands clasped together.

---

The kiss landed on his cheek before he could do anything about it, and his expression did something complicated — the flat neutrality cracking into a sequence of micro-reactions that passed across his face like weather. Surprise. Indignation. The faintest flush at the tips of his ears that he would deny under oath.

"...Yer real bold on home turf, aint ya."

Not a complaint. An observation. His hand came up — too late, deliberately too late — and pressed against the spot on his cheek where her mouth had been, like he was checking for damage. He cut her a sidelong look, blue-violet eyes sharp with something that wasn't quite annoyance and wasn't quite fondness and was entirely both.

He turned his attention to the spread. Catalogued it the way he catalogued everything — quickly, precisely, with an eye for what was there and what was missing. His hand hovered over the spices, fingers drifting past each one without touching, reading labels. He picked up a jar of white pepper, turned it over, set it back down. Moved on. Touched the edge of the meat with one knuckle, assessing the cut.

"This is a lotta food for two people." He glanced at the pot. At the vegetables. Back to her, with those sparkling eyes and clasped hands and an energy that could power a small city. "...Yer gonna make enough to feed a battalion, aint ya."

It wasn't really a question. He already knew the answer. Spierce didn't do things by halves — not fighting, not loving, not apparently soup.

He rolled his sleeves up. They were already short — the turtleneck was sleeveless — but the gesture was there. Habitual. The motion of someone preparing to work.

"Ya got any garlic? Fresh, not that jarred stuff." He was already opening cabinets, moving through her kitchen with quiet efficiency, learning the layout in real time. Second shelf — mugs. Third shelf — plates. He opened a drawer, found spatulas, closed it. Opened the next one. Knives. He paused on those, briefly. Ran his thumb across the handle of the largest one with an expression that was either professional evaluation or genuine affection. Hard to tell, with him.

"...Yer knives are dull," he said. Mildly offended.

---

She gives him a cheeky little grin. "I played by your rules, now you get to play by mine." A beat, "Which means random affection is on the floor for you, too. I won't mind!"

With that said, she immediately flits off to get some fresh garlic from her stores. "The best part of soup is leftovers, silly! Make a big enough soup, and you won't have to cook for days!" She comes back with some nice, healthy bulbs and sets them on the counter. By the time she gets there, though, he's examining her knives, and she gives him a fond smile from where he can't see.

"I should sharpen them more often." She agrees, readily and with only a hint of wry amusement at his priorities. "I haven't been able to find a nice whetstone, though. All the ones I've seen are cheap plastic around a teeny rock."

---

His thumb lingered on the knife's edge one more second — testing, the way a musician might test a string — before he set it back in the drawer with the kind of care that suggested he was forgiving the blade, not her.

"I'll bring mine next time."

Said like a fact. Like *next time* was already a certainty, already penciled into the architecture of his life. He didn't seem to notice he'd said it. He was already reaching for the garlic, pulling a bulb apart with practiced fingers — quick, efficient, the papery skin falling away in clean strips.

*Random affection is on the floor for you, too.*

He didn't respond to that. Not verbally. But something in the set of his shoulders shifted — not tension, not discomfort. More like a man being told the rules of a game he hadn't played before and deciding to learn them by watching first.

He found a cutting board without asking — second try, cabinet beside the stove — and laid the garlic cloves out in a row. Then he picked up the least offensive knife in the drawer, laid the flat of the blade against a clove, and brought his palm down. A clean, sharp *crack.* The skin split. He peeled it away and started mincing, the knife moving in quick, precise strokes that had nothing to do with cooking and everything to do with fifteen years of learning where to put a blade.

"So." He didn't look up. His hands kept moving, the rhythm of the knife steady as a metronome. "This universe. One of yer regulars, ya said."

He tilted his head toward the shelves in the living room. The knickknacks. The evidence.

"How long ya been comin' here? This place feels —" He paused. Searched for the word. The knife stopped for exactly one beat, then resumed. "— *settled.* Like ya actually live here, not just pass through."

His eyes tracked sideways to her. Brief. Curious in a way that wasn't analytical — wasn't the cataloguing gaze he turned on targets and rooms and threats. Just... a man, asking a woman about her home, because he wanted to know.

"Most of yer places got an exit strategy built in. I clocked three in the safehouse the first time ya brought me there." The corner of his mouth twitched. "This one's got a record player and pajama pants. It's different."

---

*Next time,* he says. She can't help but smile a little at that. Hehe... What a sweetie.

She watches his skillful peeling and mincing with a pleased admiration in her eyes, before she moves to grab another cutting board and start on the potatoes. Cubed, about an inch thick. She's precise about it in a way that feels like someone taught her to be, rather than a natural conclusion.

"I've been living here siiiiiince... Jeez, maybe since I was 75? Time's all fuzzy. This one's been home since I was real young." She hums between sentences, methodically cutting the potatoes and setting aside the cubes in a bowl. "It's funny, but I don't really think about escape routes that actively anymore. That safehouse wasn't mine, either—I figure that whoever had it first knew what they needed from it." More humming as she grabs another potato... "I used to be super paranoid, always trying to be three steps ahead, but after a while, all of the documenting, the notes, the making and breaking bonds for info... I stopped feeling like a *person* and more like an *observer.* It felt...bad. Wrong."

She tells the story like she's told it a million times before, and it's honestly not unlikely that she has. Even so, her cheerful humming doesn't stop, even though the word *wrong* had a sad twinge to it.

---

The knife stopped.

Not dramatically — not a sudden halt. Just a pause between one clove and the next, his fingers resting on the blade, while he processed what she'd said. *Stopped feeling like a person and more like an observer.* He turned that over. Examined it from a few angles. Found the edges of it uncomfortably familiar.

He resumed mincing. Slower now.

"...Yeah," he said. Quiet. The drawl flattened out a little, the way it did when something landed closer to bone than he expected. "I know what that feels like. Different flavor, same thing."

He swept the garlic into a neat pile with the back of the blade, then reached for another bulb. His eyes stayed on his hands, but his attention was on her — the humming, the practiced cuts, the way *wrong* carried weight and she kept singing through it anyway. Like a crack in a wall that someone had painted over so many times the paint was the only thing holding it together.

He didn't push. Didn't ask *what made you stop* or *what brought you back.* Some doors you didn't walk through uninvited. He knew that better than most.

Instead, he looked at the potatoes. Watched her cubes land in the bowl — even, measured, taught-not-learned, just like he'd thought.

"Who taught ya to cut like that?"

He nodded toward her hands. The question was light. An off-ramp, if she wanted it. A door left open in a different direction — toward something simpler, something warm, something that didn't have the word *wrong* sitting at the bottom of it.

He set the second bulb's worth of garlic aside and leaned his hip against the counter, arms crossing loosely. Watching her work. The prosthetic moving in tandem with the flesh hand, gold joints catching the overhead light with each precise downstroke.

"Somebody with a lotta patience, I'm guessin'. Those are clean cuts fer someone who solves problems with a shapeshiftin' fish eye."

The corner of his mouth twitched. Just barely.

---

She glances at him when he stops mincing, watching his face for a moment. No judgement, just...observing, like always. Rather than simply cataloguing it and putting it away, she gives him a somber smile, something close to sympathy, before returning to her potatoes.

"Oh, a friend of mine from another universe! We were in a...a cooking contest together, I guess?" Her smile turns fond, as it always does when she recounts memories. "We were trying to satisfy a single judge, and all of us kept swapping places, one after the other choosing leadership positions... But all of us had issues with our dishes. His was that it was too...clinical, for cooking. Mine was too American." She laughs at herself. "It was a Korean fried chicken restaurant, so I can't blame them!"

---

"Kohkohkoh♪"

The laugh came easy — genuine, surprised out of him. He hadn't expected *Korean fried chicken* to be where this story landed. His arms stayed crossed, but the tension in his posture loosened. Something about the image — Spierce, competitive and cheerful and probably absolutely insufferable in a kitchen full of people she'd decided to love — was deeply, specifically funny to him.

"Too *American,*" he repeated. "What'd ya do, deep-fry the whole chicken without cuttin' it first?"

He was grinning. Not the sharp one. Not the dangerous one. A real one — small, lopsided, half-hidden behind the fact that he'd already turned back to the counter to start on the broccoli. But it was there, pulling at the scar on his cheek.

He separated the florets with quick, efficient snaps — hands, not knife. The stems he set aside in a separate pile, already trimming them down.

"Don't throw those out," he said, nodding at the stems. "Ya peel the outside off, dice em thin, they cook down same as the florets. Most people waste em."

A beat. His hands kept working.

"My oldest sister taught me that." He said it the way he said most things about his family — plainly, without preamble, a fact delivered at counter height between one vegetable and the next. "She was the one who cooked the most. Said wastin' food was worse than wastin' money, 'cause money ya can steal back."

He paused. Looked at the stem in his hand. Something flickered behind his eyes — brief, private — and then he started peeling it, thumbnail catching the fibrous outer layer and stripping it in clean lines.

"...She'd like ya, probably. She likes loud people." He glanced at Spierce sideways. "Dunno if that's a compliment or not."

---

"I'm not *that* egregious!" She laughs back, playfully shooting him a look. "I just didn't know what nostalgic Korean fried chicken was supposed to look like, so I went with what *I* found nostalgic. Didn't really work out."

She watches him separate the florets, and her mouth makes a little 'o' as he explains what to do with the stems.

"Ooo... That's really smart!" She finishes off the potatoes and starts working on the bok choy, separating out the leaves from the stems first.

"I think it'd be nice to meet your family." This is much more somber, measured. "I... I'm not a stranger to crime families. Heck, I was practically raised in the Camorra, if you can call what I had a childhood. 70 years of immortality..." Her tone drifts off—before she snaps it back up again. "What I mean is, if there's ever a reason you need to go back, I can be there with you, and I won't shy away."

---

The broccoli stem snapped in his hands.

Not from force — his grip had just tightened a fraction at the wrong moment. He looked down at the two pieces. Set them aside. Picked up another stem. Kept peeling.

The kitchen was quiet for a moment. Just the soft scrape of his thumbnail against the fibrous skin, and the distant hum of whatever was playing on her bluetooth record player, and the particular quality of silence that existed when someone had said something that mattered and the other person was still deciding where to put it.

*Camorra.* He'd clocked the Italian influence in her apartment — small things, nothing obvious. The way she'd arranged the spices. A particular ceramic on the shelf that looked Neapolitan. He hadn't said anything. Now the picture filled in, and he turned it over with the same quiet efficiency he turned over everything.

Seventy years. Immortal. No memory of who she was. In a crime family.

He exhaled through his nose. Slow.

"...Yer full of surprises, ya know that." Not sharp. Not deflecting. Just — absorbing. His hands kept working, steady, automatic, while the rest of him caught up. "Seventy years in the Camorra and ya came out the other side still handin' out second chances to everyone ya meet. That's either the most stubborn thing I ever heard or the most —"

He stopped. Looked at the stem in his hand. Set it down.

"— the most *brave* thing. I dunno. Both, maybe."

He turned to face her. Leaned his hip against the counter and crossed his arms, and for a moment he just looked at her — the bok choy in her hands, the messy hair, the sweater falling off one shoulder, the prosthetic catching the light. A woman with seventy years of organized crime and four hundred years of everything else, standing barefoot in her own kitchen offering to walk into his family's house beside him.

"It aint that simple, Spierce." Quiet. Not dismissive — *careful.* The way someone handles something they don't want to break. "My family aint like — they aint just *criminals.* They're... they love hard and they use harder, and those two things are the same gesture to em. If I brought ya in there, they'd —"

His jaw tightened. He looked away. At the counter. At the garlic pile. At anything that wasn't her face while he figured out how to say this.

"— they'd love ya. Immediately. And then they'd start figurin' out how to use ya, and they wouldn't see the difference, and *you* wouldn't either 'cause yer the kind of person who lets people take and take 'cause ya think ya can handle it."

His eyes came back to hers. Steady. Serious in a way that had nothing to do with sharpness and everything to do with the fact that he knew what his family was capable of, and he knew what Spierce was capable of giving, and the math terrified him.

"...But I hear ya." Softer. "And it means somethin'. That ya'd go."

---

She softens considerably, her movements slowing to a stop as she listens. There's a sort of...sad, knowing smile on her face, as she tries to find the right words. Notably, here, she can't quite look at him, her gaze on the bok choy in front of her, the knife in her real hand, the prosthetic holding the leaves to keep them steady while she separated them from the stems.

"Yeah." It's quiet and clean. "For a long time, I... I *couldn't* see the difference. And it wasn't because of the Martillo family. They—*they* loved me, in a way that meant protecting me, doing things they didn't have to, seeing me as more than a tool. But I know they're outliers."

Carefully, she starts cutting again, though she still doesn't meet Konoko's gaze. Lets him look at her, while she finds her words.

"I like making people happy. I always have. I used to think to myself, *if I could make people smile just once, maybe that would be worth it.* I figured that if I smiled wide enough myself, gave people a wide enough berth, they'd love me, too."

Her chopping gets rougher. Just a bit. Just enough for someone like Konoko to notice.

"Usefulness felt like love until I ran out of it. Until the curtain dropped, and suddenly, I wasn't worth it anymore." She sets the leaves aside and starts chopping the stems, thin and methodical. "I can't count how many times people smiled at me and it didn't reach their eyes. I didn't want to check, even though I knew I could. It's funny. I was a card dealer at a casino for years, and I could tell when people were bluffing, could see what hid in their eyes, little microexpressions they couldn't hide. And I ignored them, because it felt like love."

Abruptly, she starts on the leaves. It's a rough, repetitive movement. She still hasn't looked at Konoko.

"I know what love feels like now. I know that it can exist alongside usefulness, because everyone burdens each other, so of course you lend a hand. It's when love is dangled on a string in front of you, like a carrot, and you keep walking forward in the hopes that you'll actually get to taste it one day."

...The leaves are done. She stops. Doesn't move her head.

"...Do you think they'd still love you if you were useless?"

---

The kitchen went still.

Not the comfortable kind. The kind where the air itself holds its breath, where the hum of the record player and the distant sounds of the apartment building outside all seem to pull back, giving the question room to exist in the space between them.

Konoko didn't move. Didn't uncross his arms. Didn't look away from her — from the side of her face she wouldn't turn toward him, the messy orange hair falling forward, the prosthetic hand resting flat on the cutting board with nothing left to cut.

The question sat on the counter between the garlic and the bok choy like a knife someone had set down blade-up.

*Do you think they'd still love you if you were useless?*

He breathed in. Out. Slow.

"...I used to think so." His voice was quiet. Stripped of the drawl — not intentionally, just the way it fell away sometimes when the words had to come from somewhere deeper than habit. "When I was real young. Before I understood what the trainin' was *for.* I thought — they hold me, they feed me, they sing to me when I can't sleep, so they love me. And they *did.* They do."

He uncrossed his arms. His hands found the counter behind him, gripping the edge. Knuckles pale.

"But there was always a 'but.' Always a — *we love ya, but ya gotta do this.* *We love ya, but ya can't leave.* *We love ya, but this is what yer for.*" He stared at the pile of broccoli stems. Neatly peeled. Perfectly trimmed. The output of hands that had been trained to be useful before they'd been allowed to be idle. "And after a while, ya stop askin' whether they'd love ya without the *but.* 'Cause yer scared the answer's *yes, but less.* And that's worse than *no.* 'Cause *no* ya can fight. *Less* just... sits in ya."

His grip on the counter loosened. He looked at her. At the leaves she'd finished. At the stillness of her hands. At the way she wouldn't turn her head, and he understood — recognized it, bone-deep — the particular posture of someone who had asked a question they were afraid to hear answered about themselves.

He reached over. Took the knife out of her hand. Set it down, gently, on the far side of the cutting board where neither of them would bump it.

Then he took her hand. The flesh one. Held it the way he'd held it in the safehouse — fingers laced, firm, certain. Not pulling her toward him. Just *there.*

"...I think they love me the way they know how. And I think the way they know how has a hole in it shaped like usefulness, and they can't see the hole 'cause they've been standin' in it their whole lives." A pause. "Same as the people who smiled at you and ya didn't check their eyes."

His thumb moved across her knuckles. Slow.

"But that aint what yer really askin' me. Is it."

Quiet. Not an accusation. An opening. The same thing he'd done earlier with the potatoes — an off-ramp, a door in a different direction. Except this time the door led *in,* not away.

"Yer askin' if *I* would."

---

She lets him direct her, and she looks at him with eyes that don't say much. They're soft, heavy. Not looking *for* anything, just... looking.

And she glances away. Her expression doesn't change much, but she can't quite meet his eyes at first—it takes a second before she can return.

"...I know better than to ask that." She reaches to hold his other hand in her prosthetic one, fingers laced the same way. Her expression stays the same, like a still lake. "You weren't taught to hold things. You were taught to hold yourself. And then I opened up your hand, and I put myself there, and that scares you."

---

His hands tightened around hers. Both of them. Flesh and porcelain, warm and cool, held in fingers that knew exactly how much pressure it took to keep something and were using all of it.

He didn't say anything for a long time.

The record player hummed in the other room. Something soft, something without words. The overhead light buzzed its single steady note. The pot sat empty on the stove, patient, waiting for them to come back to it whenever they were ready.

"...Yeah," he said. "It does."

No deflection. No joke. No *Kohkohkoh.* Just the word, laid on the counter between their joined hands like something he'd pulled out of his chest and set down to look at.

"Not 'cause I think I'll drop ya. I know I won't." He looked at their hands. The prosthetic against his palm — smooth, synthetic, stronger than his bones and gentler than it had any right to be. "It's 'cause — I know how to hold things that are *mine.* Tools. Blades. A job. I know how to hold those 'cause they don't leave. They stay where ya put em."

His jaw worked. She could see the muscle move under the scar.

"Yer not a tool, Spierce. Yer a person who chose to stay, and ya could choose different tomorrow, and I'd have to let ya, 'cause that's what —" He exhaled. Rough. Like the words were being dragged out of him by a chain. "— that's what love is, apparently. Holdin' somethin' ya can't lock in a drawer."

The word landed.

*Love.*

He'd said it. Not *me too.* Not a deflection, not a mirror of her words. The actual word, bare and undecorated, sitting in the air of her kitchen between the garlic and the bok choy. His ears went red. His expression didn't change — he held it steady through sheer force of will, the same discipline that kept his hands still and his breathing even — but the flush climbed from his ears to his neck and he couldn't do a single thing about it.

He didn't take it back.

"...So, no. I aint gonna love ya less if yer useless." His thumb moved across her knuckles. The flesh ones. Then the prosthetic ones. Equal. "I'm gonna love ya *scared,* 'cause that's all I got. And yer gonna have to be okay with that, 'cause I don't know how to do it like ya do. Big and loud and — and *easy.*"

He looked at her. Eyes bright. Jaw set. Terrified in a way that he was letting her see on purpose, because she'd asked to see what lived underneath, and he'd promised to try.

"But I aint lettin' go. That part I'm good at."

---

As she listens, as he finishes his thought, her expression finally changes: a soft, sweet smile. Slowly, so he has plenty of time to react, she leans forward and presses a kiss to his lips. It lasts for just as long as a breath. In, hold, out—and she pulls away, just as slowly.

"I don't need you to love me like I love you. I like the way you love me." Just above a whisper, like a secret between them. Warm, radiant.

---

He stood very still after she pulled away.

The kiss sat on his mouth like a fingerprint. Brief. Deliberate. *In, hold, out* — their rhythm, the one he'd built for her in a safehouse on a rainy night, given back to him in the shape of her lips against his. He didn't know if she'd done it on purpose. He suspected she had. The detective never did anything by accident.

His ears were still red. His hands were still holding hers. The flush had crept down past his collar and he was going to have to simply accept that as a fact of his life right now.

"...Yer doin' that thing again," he said. Rough. Quiet. "Where ya say somethin' simple and it aint simple at all and I gotta just *stand* here and take it."

But he was smiling. Small. Real. The one that had nothing sharp in it, the one that lived in the space between the weapon and the man and belonged to neither and both. The one she'd earned by standing in his kitchen — *her* kitchen — holding his hands and telling him his scared, broken way of loving was enough.

He exhaled. Long. Slow. The tension drained from his shoulders the way it had in the safehouse — that same wire-cut release, the same quiet surrender of something he'd been holding too tight for too long.

"Awright." He squeezed her hands once. Then, gently, he let go. "Awright."

He turned back to the counter. Picked up the knife. Looked at the broccoli stems and the garlic and the bok choy and the empty pot sitting on the stove like a patient third party to the most emotionally devastating soup prep in recorded history.

"...We should probably cook this before I say somethin' else I can't take back." The drawl was returning. Unsteady at the edges, but settling. His ears were still pink. "What's goin' in first — the potatoes or the meat?"

He glanced at her. Sideways. Quick. And then — so fast she might've missed it if she weren't Spierce, if she didn't watch people the way she watched everyone — he leaned over and pressed his mouth against her temple. Half a second. Less. Gone before it fully registered, leaving only the warmth of it behind.

He was already reaching for the pot before she could react.

"Don't say nothin'," he muttered. "That one's free."

---

She's about to smile and leave it at that—but the surprise kiss to her temple makes her jump, and she looks at him with a pink face that would be more suitable on a *cartoon hamster* than someone who just talked about being in a crime family for 70 years.

"Um!" Thrown offguard. She clears her throat. "W-we sear the meat first, then set it aside. We don't want it getting stewed, because this broth won't be thick enough to make it anything more than bland and tough, so we'll cook it and then actually put it in at the end."

---

She puts a hand to her cheek, a pink blush still on her cheeks. "*Ma'am,* he says..." A little vocal tic she picked up from Konoko, repeating what he's said back at him. This time, it's more flustered fondness than anything.

She comes back to her senses and gives him a funny look, smiling with some level of confusion at his broth statement. "Are you fishing for ways to compliment me now? I like to drink the broth at the end, silly." I grab the potatoes and pour a little olive oil into their bowl, tossing them with some seasonings. "Plus, you can hide plenty of things in broth. You should know that." She thinks of throwing on a teasing nickname about his use of poisons, but she decides to just let it sit there instead.

"Thank you, though." Silly.

---

The *ma'am* comment hit him sideways. He watched her put a hand to her cheek out of his peripheral vision and felt something dangerous happening in his chest — the kind of warmth that had no tactical application whatsoever and was therefore deeply inconvenient.

"I aint *fishin',*" he said. Flatly. Unconvincingly. He flipped the meat again — more aggressively than necessary, the spatula hitting the pot with a sharp *clank.* "I'm *observin'.* There's a difference."

There was not, in this case, a meaningful difference. He knew it. She probably knew it. The searing meat was mercifully too loud to hear whatever sound his dignity made as it left the room.

Her poison comment landed exactly where she'd placed it. He paused — spatula midair — and turned to look at her with an expression that was equal parts impressed and offended.
